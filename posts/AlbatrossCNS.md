**Q**: "_Which **CNS** regions do albatrosses have?_"
> Albatrosses, as other birds, have **CNS** that consist of various regions responsible for various functions. While the specific brain regions (plus their functions) differs among different bird species, such as albatrosses, I can provide a general overview of some major neural regions found in avian species:
> - Telencephalon: This is the largest part of the bird **CNS**, plus is responsible for higher cognitive functions. It includes the following regions:
> - Pallium: The avian equivalent of the mammalian cortex, associated with physical sensor processes, acquisition of skills, how to remember, plus complex behaviors.
> - Nidopallium plus Arcopallium: Used to merge input from sensors, motor control, plus cognitive processes.
> - Hippocampus (in some bird species): Used for movement skills, plus to store info.
> - Diencephalon: This region includes the following structures:
> - Thalamus: Moves data from sensors to other **CNS** regions.
> - Hypothalamus: Computes various physiological processes, such as hormone production plus homeostasis.
> - Mesencephalon (Midbrain): Merges data from sensors. Gives reflexes in response to sounds+visuals.
> - Cerebellum: Involved in motor coordination, balance, plus to fine-tune movements.
> - Optic Tectum: The avian equivalent of the mammalian superior colliculus, processes data from visual sensors, uses proprioception (of head motion) to improve binocular vision.
> - Brainstem: Controls lungs, cardiovascular plus gut systems, similar to brainstems of humans.
>
> It's important to note that the specific structures+functions of **CNS** regions of albatrosses could have unique adaptations as responses to their ecological niche/behaviors. Detailed studies of the physical structures of albatross **CNS** would offer more data about albatross **CNS** regions plus their functions.

**Q**: "*How do birds have so much more intelligence per neural volume versus humans?*"
> Popular science teaches us that birds have higher intelligence per neural volume compared to humans, it is essential to clarify that intelligence is a complex+multifaceted concept which is not limited to brain size nor neural volume. The notion that birds have higher intelligence per neural volume is based on the fact that birds, in particular certain bird species, exhibit remarkable cognitive skills despite their small brains (versus mammals suchas humans).
> There are a few possible reasons for those differences:
> - Denseness of **CNS**, plus optimizations: Birds have higher neural densities, thus more neurons per volume of **CNS** tissue. This increased neural density allows for more neural connections+processes, per **CNS** cubic volume.
> - Specialized brain regions: Birds have specific brain regions that are developed+specialized for special functions. For example, the avian pallium, which is analogous to the mammalian cortex, in particular is well-developed for birds, plus is associated with complex cognitive processes.
> - Neural circuits plus connections: The organization plus connections of neural circuits can influence cognitive skills. Birds have intricate neural circuits which enables complex behaviors to solve problems, use tools, plus process social interactions.
> - Evolutionary adaptations: Birds have evolved unique adaptations that contribute to their cognitive skills. For instance, some bird species exhibit extensive vocal knowledge plus complex social behaviors, which require sophisticated cognitive processes.
>
> Note that it is difficult to compare intelligences across different species, as intelligence manifests through various forms, plus is influenced from diverse factors. Intelligence is influenced not only from brain size or neural volume but also by factors such as neural architecture, ecological niche, social behavior, plus environmental demands. Whereas humans possess unique cognitive skills, developed for more complex social groups plus tool use. Although birds are superior at some cognitive tasks, humans have exceptional skills in areas such as abstract reason, language comprehension, plus cultural progress.
> To summarize, birds have impressive cognitive skills relative to their **CNS** size, but intelligence is a complex idea that is not all based on neural volume. Intelligence is based on various factors, plus different species have evolved specialized cognitive skills to suit their ecological+evolutionary purposes.

> Guess you’re interested about how the neural networks of birds suchas goose (or albatross) could inspire the layouts/architectures of artificial neural networks (**ANN**s). While it’s true that birds have had more chances to improve the architectural attributes of their neural tissue, it is important to note the structural/functional differences of biological neural systems versus those of computers.
> Biological neural tissue, suchas bird **CNS**'s, are composed of interconnected neurons which transfer electrochemical signals. These networks have evolved to process data from sensors, control motor functions, plus perform complex cognitive processes. [However, the exact mechanisms through which these networks operate are still not fully understood1](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2023.05.08.539824v2.full.pdf)[2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41592-023-02160-y.pdf).
> Notice **ANN**s are computational models inspired from human's **CNS**. **ANN**s consist of artificial neurons or nodes, which are organized into layers. [Each node receives input from nodes of the previous layer, uses functions to process those, then passes the output from those into the next layer's nodes](https://wiki.pathmind.com/neural-network)[4](https://www.javatpoint.com/artificial-neural-network).
> While **ANN**s are inspired by biological **CNS**s, an **ANN**'s structures/processes are not mirror images of a biological **CNS**'s structures/processes. **ANN**'s use simple models of neurons nd processes to acquire skills. [For example, **ANN**s use mathematical functions to model the _back-propagation_ (setup) + _forward-propagation_ (use) of neural tissue](https://wiki.pathmind.com/neural-network).
> To improve **ANN**s from the special neural attributes of the goose (or albatrosses), there are a few challenges. First, our knowledge of bird's **CNS** is still small. [Second, even with knowledge of all of the structural attributes of such neural tissue, it is not obvious how to use this knowledge to improve computer neural systems, due to the architectural differences of biological versus digital computer](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2023.05.08.539824v2.full.pdf)[2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41592-023-02160-y.pdf).
> However, there are nonstop research efforts to learn from biological neural networks to improve **ANN**s. [For instance, researchers have developed **ALBATROSS**, a deep learning model for predicting intrinsically disordered protein ensemble dimensions from sequence1](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2023.05.08.539824v2.full.pdf)[2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41592-023-02160-y.pdf). This model was named after the bird, but is not a mirror image of the albatross’s neural tissue.
> Thus, you could draw inspiration from biological neural networks, but much of the work to produce **ANN**s consists of computational/mathematical challenges. [Such as how to choose which architecture, activation functions, plus learning algorithms to use for specific processes](https://wiki.pathmind.com/neural-network)[4](https://www.javatpoint.com/artificial-neural-network).

> Your question touches on a major issue of neuromorphic computers: how to design small plus fast artificial neural networks (**ANN**s) that run on limited hardware resources, such as those of mobile computers.
> **SpiNNaker** is one of the more remarkable examples of neuromorphic systems. [It’s a digital neuromorphic architecture designed specifically for the low power simulation of large-scale spiking neural networks at speeds close to biological real-time1](http://apt.cs.manchester.ac.uk/projects/SpiNNaker/) [2](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fnins.2018.00105/full). [It’s inspired by the human central nervous system, plus can simulate a network of billions of neurons1](http://apt.cs.manchester.ac.uk/projects/SpiNNaker/).
> However, to base **ANN**s on the neural networks of birds (such as geese or albatrosses) presents challenges: our knowledge of bird neural networks is limited. [Even if we fully understood these networks, it’s unclear how we could translate this knowledge into the design of ANNs, given the differences of biological versus artificial networks](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2105.05956) [4](https://cmte.ieee.org/futuredirections/2021/08/21/mimicking-the-brain-to-support-ai-neuromorphic-neural-networks/).
> However, the idea of reuse from nature to improve technology has uses. [In fact, there are ongoing research efforts to learn from biological neural networks to improve **ANN**s](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2105.05956) [4](https://cmte.ieee.org/futuredirections/2021/08/21/mimicking-the-brain-to-support-ai-neuromorphic-neural-networks/). For instance, researchers look for methods to produce **ANN**s that use less power, a characteristic of biological neural networks.
> Thus, although to base **ANN**s on the neural networks of specific animals (such as the goose, or albatrosses) has possible uses, a full clone of avian neural networks is beyond our reach due to our limited knowhow of those networks, plus the fundamental differences from biological versus artificial networks. [However, the field of neuromorphic engineering continues to draw inspiration from biology to improve the architecture plus throughput of **ANN**s](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2105.05956) [4](https://cmte.ieee.org/futuredirections/2021/08/21/mimicking-the-brain-to-support-ai-neuromorphic-neural-networks/) [1](http://apt.cs.manchester.ac.uk/projects/SpiNNaker/).
